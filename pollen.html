🌲 Juniper / Eastern Red Cedar
◌ Seasonal
High 9.0/10
One of the most potent tree allergens in the Midwest. Triggers strong reactions in nearly all tree-allergic people. The main cause of "cedar fever" in Indiana and Missouri — and it peaks before most people realize spring has started.
Severe allergen 📅 December – April ⚠️ Cross-reactive
🍁 Maple
◌ Seasonal
High 9.0/10
One of the first trees to release pollen in Indiana — often while the tree still looks bare. Highly allergenic. Most people don't suspect it because the trees don't look like they're doing anything yet.
Severe allergen 📅 February – April ⚠️ Cross-reactive
🌳 Elm
◌ Seasonal
Med-High 7.5/10
Moderate allergen that peaks in early spring alongside maple and juniper. Common in Indiana and adds to total tree pollen load during the heaviest overlap weeks.

🌿 Ash
◌ Seasonal
Medium 5.0/10
Moderate allergen, common in Indiana river valleys. Cross-reacts with olive trees. Often underestimated because it overlaps with peak birch season and gets lost in the noise.

🌳 Oak
◌ Seasonal
Low-Med 2.5/10
Produces enormous quantities of pollen — the yellow-green dust coating cars in April is usually oak. Moderate allergenicity per grain, but sheer volume makes it significant.
Moderate allergen 📅 April – May ⚠️ Cross-reactive
🌿 Cottonwood / Poplar
◌ Seasonal
Low-Med 2.5/10
The white fluffy "snow" in spring is seed casing — not pollen. The actual allergenic pollen is released before the fluff appears. Many people blame the visible fluff and miss the real trigger window.
